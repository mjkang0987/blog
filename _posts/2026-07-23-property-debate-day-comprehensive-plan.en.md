At 10 AM today (July 23), President Lee Jae-myung personally chairs a "National Housing Debate." Some 140 people — government officials, experts and citizen representatives — gather in one place to openly discuss property and transaction tax reform, loan regulation and housing supply over 100 minutes. The president will take in the views raised at the ministry-by-ministry debates held on July 14–16 and reflect them in a comprehensive plan and tax reform package due in late July to early August. With Seoul apartment prices up for 75 straight weeks, the session draws extra attention. 🏠

**TL;DR**

- Today at 10 AM, a presidential housing debate opens (100 minutes, 140 participants).
- The agenda splits three ways: property/transaction tax, loan regulation and housing supply.
- The outcome feeds a comprehensive plan and tax reform package due late July to early August.

## 🗣️ Who Is at Today's Debate, and What Do They Discuss?

The debate is built around the president hearing citizens directly and folding their views into the coming comprehensive plan. It runs for about 100 minutes from 10 AM today, with 140 people representing government, experts and the public. The 75 citizen representatives were drawn broadly — from industry and associations, influencers, real-estate and parenting-café operators, licensed brokers, and civic and youth groups. The idea is to capture the voices of people who actually experience the market, not only a narrow circle of experts.

The program runs in order: the president's opening remarks, a report on pre-collected opinions, sector presentations, open debate, and closing remarks. The presentations split into three tracks. Supply goes to Professor Jin Mi-yun of Myongji University, finance to Kim Young-do, research fellow at the Korea Institute of Finance, and taxation to Professor Kang Sung-hoon of Hanyang University. In effect, the three pillars of housing policy — supply, finance and taxation — are all on the table.

## ⚖️ What Are the Three Core Issues?

The backbone of today's discussion is property and transaction tax, loan regulation and housing supply. On taxation, the key question is how to rework the holding tax and transaction tax. A proposal to shift the holding tax from a "number of homes" basis to a "home value" basis emerged as a flashpoint at the earlier tax debate, and possible easing of the transaction tax is on the table too. On finance, the focus is how much to tighten or loosen loan rules; on supply, how to secure the volume needed to support genuine demand.

This grand debate did not appear out of nowhere. Over three days on July 14–16, the government first held ministry-by-ministry public debates in order — the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport (supply), the Financial Services Commission (finance) and the Ministry of Economy and Finance (taxation). Today is the wrap-up, with the president pulling those discussions together.

## 📈 Why Does This Debate Matter Now?

Today's discussion carries added weight because Seoul home prices keep rising despite regulation. By Korea Real Estate Board tallies, Seoul apartment sale prices rose for 75 straight weeks through the third week of July, with the recent weekly gain holding around 0.30%. By district, Seongbuk-gu led at 0.49%, followed by Guro-gu. Even with regulation in place, the momentum has shifted toward northern Seoul and the outskirts, showing little sign of cooling.

Ultimately, the direction set today feeds straight into policy. The government has signaled it will unveil a comprehensive plan bundling supply, finance and taxation as early as late July, and by early August at the latest. A tax reform package is due around the same time. How far today's debate reflects public opinion will shape the strength and direction of that plan.

## ✅ The Takeaway

Today's debate is where the president personally gathers the conclusions of the past week's ministry debates, and a preview of the comprehensive plan to come. With Seoul apartment prices up for 75 straight weeks, the mere fact that property and transaction tax, loans and supply were addressed together will inevitably draw market attention. That said, today is more about gathering opinions than reaching conclusions, so the actual shape of policy is best confirmed at the late-July to early-August plan announcement. Worth watching: the direction the presenters lay out on supply, finance and taxation, and how much weight lands in the president's closing remarks.
