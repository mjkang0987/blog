Seoul apartment prices have now risen for 72 straight weeks, led by the city's outer districts. In the weekly housing report for the fourth week of June (as of June 22), released by the Korea Real Estate Board (REB) on June 25, Seoul sale prices rose 0.30%, a wider gain than the previous week's 0.27%. Dobong-gu stood out with a 0.46% weekly jump—its largest in 7 years and 9 months. The rental market ran even hotter: Seoul jeonse (lump-sum deposit lease) rents climbed 0.35%, the steepest in 12 years and 8 months. It was a week in which buying demand, once concentrated in the Gangnam belt, visibly spread to outer areas such as Nowon, Dobong and Gangbuk to sidestep regulation.

**TL;DR**
- Seoul apartment sale prices rose +0.30% in week 4 of June (vs. +0.27% prior), a 72nd straight weekly gain with a wider increase.
- The outskirts led — Dobong +0.46% (biggest in 7 years 9 months), Seongbuk and Guro +0.41%, Dongdaemun +0.38%.
- Seoul jeonse +0.35%, the highest in 12 years 8 months. Dongtan cooled to +1.65% (from +2.22%); regional Korea was flat (0.00%) for a 4th week.

## How much did Seoul prices rise this week?

Seoul apartment sale prices rose 0.30% in week 4 of June, a wider gain than the prior week's 0.27%, extending the streak to 72 consecutive weeks. The REB said demand held up across the city, with buyer inquiries and above-asking deals concentrating on well-located complexes and those with redevelopment expectations, lifting prices across all of Seoul.

The greater metropolitan area as a whole rose 0.21%, unchanged from the previous week. As Seoul widened its gains, Gyeonggi and Incheon eased off, so the metro average held steady.

## Why are outer districts like Nowon-Dobong-Gangbuk leading?

The key story this week is that the center of gravity of the rally shifted from the Gangnam belt to Seoul's outskirts. Dobong-gu rose the most in the city at 0.46%. With buying interest gathering around Changdong and Banghak station-area complexes, it logged its biggest weekly gain since the second week of September 2018 (0.52%)—7 years and 9 months ago. Seongbuk-gu (0.41%), Guro-gu (0.41%), Dongdaemun-gu (0.38%) and Jung-gu (0.37%) followed.

Underlying this outer-district strength is the so-called "balloon effect." As high-priced Gangnam complexes became harder to enter amid lending curbs and price burdens, both end-user and "gap investment" (buying with a tenant's jeonse deposit) demand migrated to cheaper outer complexes with improving transit. Demand is concentrating on complexes near stations or with redevelopment prospects.

## Why did jeonse jump to a 12.5-year high?

Seoul's jeonse market is climbing even faster than sale prices. In week 4 of June, Seoul jeonse rose 0.35%, a wider gain than the prior week's 0.30%, marking the highest weekly increase in 12 years and 8 months. Metro-area jeonse held at 0.21%, level with the previous week.

The strength reflects both tenants delaying purchases to stay in rentals and a shortage of new supply. Wait-and-see demand—watching whether prices climb further—is crowding into jeonse, while fewer new move-ins have left some districts short of rental listings. Rising jeonse can lower the cost of gap investment and push sale prices back up, so sales and rentals are increasingly feeding off each other.

## Have Dongtan and southern Gyeonggi cooled?

Southern Gyeonggi, which had surged, took a breather. Gyeonggi rose 0.19%, and Hwaseong Dongtan—prized as a back-area of the semiconductor belt—rose 1.65%, still the highest nationwide but narrower than the prior week's 2.22%. Areas like Seongnam Bundang, which had spiked, also saw their pace ease somewhat.

By contrast, regional (non-metro) Korea stayed flat at 0.00% for a fourth straight week. Alongside the gap between the metro area and the regions, there is also a split within the metro area itself—outer Seoul leading while southern Gyeonggi slows.

## Bottom line — a rally driven by the outskirts and jeonse, with variables still ahead

The week 4 housing market can be summed up as "a rally driven by outer Seoul and jeonse." Seoul sale prices rose 0.30% for a 72nd straight week, the outskirts led by Dobong (biggest in 7 years 9 months) drove gains via the balloon effect, and jeonse hit a 12.5-year high at 0.35%. At the same time, southern Gyeonggi slowed and the regions stayed flat, so the breadth and texture of the rally vary by area.

Two points are worth watching. First, a structure in which the outskirts and jeonse prop up gains—where sales and rentals stoke each other—leaves room for short-term overheating, which could clash with the government's household-debt and lending regulations. Second, some leading indicators are flashing signs of a possible slowdown ahead, so whether the current momentum lasts into the second half hinges on new supply, the rate path and regulatory variables. Rather than reacting to a single week's number, it helps to watch sales, jeonse and transaction volumes together.
